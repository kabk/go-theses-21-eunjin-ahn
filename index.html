Abstract



I loved horror movies since I was a kid. But from a certain point, it was so disturbing to watch horror films so I stopped and it took a long time to be able to watch them again. In horror movies mostly women are hit, raped and killed or they are just a monster. Is it possible to be a feminist and horror film fan at the same time?

Over time, the way of illustrating female characters in horror movies is quite different from the past. In this thesis, I classified the way women are consumed in horror movies into four categories. Each chapter will provide direct examples through specific films for each type, and I will observe the comparison the way female characters are portrayed in past horror films versus the way female characters are portrayed in modern horror films. I will analyze the gaze we look at horror movies and create an opportunity to think about new directions for horror movies to move forward. Furthermore, I would like to ask what attitude and responsibility we should have as artists through this research.

From the 1970s to late 1980, it was the prime of slasher horror films and the time when the ‚ÄòFinal Girl' was highly active on the screen. ‚ÄòFinal Girl‚Äô refers to the last girl(s) or woman alive to confront the killer, the one who left to tell the story. The Texas Chain Saw Massacre(1974), Halloween(1978), Alien(1979), Friday the 13th(1980), A Nightmare on Elm street(1984) and Scream(1996) are typical final girl horror films. This trope is by Carol J. Clover in her book Men, Women, and Chainsaws: Gender in the Modern Horror Film(1992). Final Girl is an iconic horror trope and usually Final Girl characters are considered as symbols of strength, bravery and resilience. However interestingly, despite all the appearances in completely different films, final girls share similar characteristics. Usually they are unisex named young girls on the edge of their teens and either uninterested in men or virgin. They are mature or smarter than their peers and in the end of the movie, they survives alone in a dark bloody place and wait for the killer with preparation for revenge of their friends or family. Clover describes the character as such:
‚ÄúShe is the one who encounters the mutilated bodies of her friends and perceives the full extent of the preceding horror and of her own peril; who is chased, cornered, wounded; whom we see scream, stagger, fall, rise, and scream again.‚Äù

Halloween (1978)

The movie that best explains the Final Girl theory is Halloween, which can be said to have set the final girl formula with the most iconic Final Girl, Laurie. On October 31, 1963, on Halloween night in the small town of Haddonfield, Illinois, six-year-old Michael Myers stabs his older sister Judith to death with a kitchen knife, and is incarcerated at Smith's Grove sanitarium. Fifteen years later, on October 30, 1978, Michael's psychiatrist Dr. Samuel Loomis arrive at the sanitarium to escort Michael to court. Michael escapes by stealing their car and returns to Haddonfield, killing a mechanic. The next day on Halloween, Michael stalks high school student Laurie Strode throughout the day and she notices, but her friends Annie and Lynda dismiss her concerns. Later that night, Michael kills Laurie's friends one by one. Eventually Laurie becomes a final girl who remains alone and struggles with Michael who wants to kill her too. Meanwhile, Loomis witnessed Laurie was making children ran away to another house down the street. Directly he chased after Laurie and Michael and at the peak of their struggle, Loomis shoots Michael. Barely saved and terrored Laurie asks Loomis if Michael was a ‚ÄòBogeyman‚Äô and Loomis replies, ‚ÄúAs a matter of fact, it was.‚Äù Then he looks down over the balcony to check Michael, but he is already gone. The film ends with a view of Myers House, where Michael committed his first murder.

But despite all of the problems, there's a funny part of the Final Girl theory. Since there is no typical ‚Äòstrong‚Äô male character in the film, the final girls become masculinized power icons in the horror genre. In modern horror films, the classic and typical Final Girls mentioned above are disappearing. The existence of the Final Girl itself has not disappeared, but it exists in a new and different way. Thomasin of The Witch(2015) is a good example. Set in 17th century New England, the film shows a very conservative and patriarchal Puritan family. Threatening Thomasin throughout the film is her family, more than witches. For no reason, all the arrows are turn to their eldest daughter, Thomasin. They blame her for her brother's mysterious death, considering sending her to another house and her father even locks her in a barn. Her family firmly believes she is a witch and she is accused of witchcraft throughout the whole movie. In the end of the film, she becomes the Final Girl who last survived among the cursed families, make a deal with Satan and joins the coven. She had made her choice with her own will, away from the family and religion that abused her. Thomasin denies the rule that the Final Girl must always be pure and be a ‚Äòhuman‚Äô against evil. She face her witchiness and accepts her desire to live freely from the patriarchal society. You‚Äôre Next(2011) also features a fresh character who denies the existing Final Girl trope as the main character. Erin's boyfriend's family gathering is attacked by unidentified men in animal masks, and at the moment of desperation, Erin begins to fight back. The villains of the movie quickly become Erin‚Äôs ‚Äòvictims,‚Äô and Erin knocks the villains down in her own way, one by one. What makes this film different from other Final Girl tropes is that the film is not focusing on her screaming and being hurt by killers. The film focuses on her showing formidable power against the villains and beastly punishing them. Audiences can feel the cool pleasure through Erin's actions and since she is a final girl who has more common ground with killers than the existing final girls, she completely collapses the existing Final Girl trope. Like these examples, the Final Girls in modern horror movies are different from the past with many potentials, and they can bring us something that is lacking in other genres. Horror genre and final girl are the symbols of female-led franchises, and clearly we are learning from the past and creating new types of final girls that have been modified and developed for the new era. This is why we should not miss our interest in the Final Girls on the screen, even if opinions about final girl trope are divided.
